About ZULFR

ZULFR is a research and standards organization focused on the intersection of formal logic, safety engineering, and artificial intelligence. Founded on the principle that governance must precede capability, ZULFR develops frameworks that treat AI outputs as high-stakes artifacts requiring the same rigor as safety-critical software systems.

Our work spans three pillars: theoretical foundations published as peer-reviewed research, practical standards embodied in our governance frameworks, and applied certification methodology for organizations deploying AI in regulated domains.

ZULFR does not build general-purpose AI systems. We build the infrastructure that makes AI systems trustworthy.

Functional Safety is Not a Snapshot

Mosaic aging hazard modeling for dynamic safety systems and continuous-time safety classification.

⌄

This paper challenges the prevailing assumption that functional safety can be assessed at a single point in time. Drawing on mosaic aging theory from materials science, we propose a continuous-time hazard model in which safety classifications degrade dynamically as system components age, interact, and drift from their certified states.

The central argument is that snapshot-based safety assessments — the dominant paradigm in IEC 61508 and ISO 26262 — systematically underestimate risk in long-lifecycle systems. We formalize a hazard accumulation function and demonstrate its tractability for real-time monitoring in embedded AI controllers.

Technical Standard DAIS-10
DAIS-10 Governance Doctrine

Risk-theoretic dominance replacing probabilistic thresholds in data admissibility protocols.

⌄

The DAIS-10 Governance Doctrine formalizes the concept of semantic admissibility as a foundational constraint in AI decision pipelines. Rather than relying on probabilistic confidence thresholds — which can be gamed and lack formal grounding — DAIS-10 introduces risk-theoretic dominance as the primary criterion for data acceptance.

A data element is admissible if and only if its semantic fingerprint is dominated by the authorized reference class under the current governance context. This approach decouples data quality from model confidence, enabling governance to be applied upstream of inference rather than as a post-hoc filter.

Working Paper
Semantic Integrity in Executive AI

Formal measurement systems for governance-first AI workflows in executive decision contexts.

⌄

As AI systems are deployed in executive decision support roles — advising on capital allocation, regulatory strategy, and organizational risk — the absence of formal integrity guarantees creates unquantified liability. This working paper proposes a measurement framework for semantic integrity that can be independently audited and certified.

We define semantic integrity operationally as the preservation of authorized meaning across the full transformation chain from raw input to decision recommendation. Integrity degrades when transformations introduce unauthorized semantic shifts — a phenomenon we term semantic drift — and we provide formal detection criteria.
